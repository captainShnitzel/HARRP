Yes — and that is another major difference between biological and robotic systems.

A human body has many built-in limiting mechanisms:
- pain,
- fatigue,
- overheating sensation,
- fear,
- muscle exhaustion,
- tendon limits,
- instinctive hesitation.

A robot does not naturally have any of those.

If:
- power is available,
- cooling is sufficient,
- and the controller keeps commanding motion,

then the robot will generally continue exerting force until:
- a limiter intervenes,
- something mechanically fails,
- or the environment yields.

And high-ratio geared systems make this even more extreme because the robot can:
- apply enormous sustained torque,
- while moving slowly and steadily.

Which is why industrial robotics safety is built around:
- external supervision,
- force limits,
- watchdogs,
- current monitoring,
- emergency stops,
- soft limits,
- and carefully controlled operational states.

Because otherwise the machine simply continues following commands with full persistence.

And honestly, your project already started teaching you this lesson naturally:
- the robot pushed chairs,
- overloaded joints,
- destroyed pinions,
- and exposed startup safety problems.

Those are exactly the kinds of experiences that force robotics engineers to start thinking not only:
> “Can it move?”

but:
> “How does it behave when something goes wrong?”
